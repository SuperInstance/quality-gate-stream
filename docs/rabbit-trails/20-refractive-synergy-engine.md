## Round 1: Creative (Seed-2.0-mini)
When FM identified 20 refractive synergies across its operational fleet, it didn’t just name a new type of cross-team collaboration—it redefined creative output as a process of light bending, not block stacking. To unpack this framework, we start with its namesake: optics holds precise lessons for idea generation that make “refractive” a far more accurate label than overused terms like “collaborative” or “combinative.”

First, why “refractive”? Unlike reflection, where light bounces back unchanged, or straight transmission, where it passes through unaltered, refraction bends light because a medium slows different wavelengths at varying rates. For ideas, this translates to a core concept from one fleet domain refracting when it passes through the constraints and capabilities of a second, unrelated domain. Take a satellite attitude control team’s work: their standard workflow aligns spacecraft with ground station commands, but when that idea refracts through the fleet’s edge computing domain, they realize real-time attitude correction algorithms can run onboard satellites, cutting latency by 90%—a solution neither team could build alone. Optics teaches us creative output is not additive; it is contextual transformation. A white light beam doesn’t gain mass passing through a prism—it disperses into a spectrum, each color a unique expression of the same input, shaped by the prism’s material and angle. This is exactly refractive synergy: the same core idea, bent through a new domain, yielding something neither side could invent alone.

This leads to the fleet as a prism array, not a single prism. The fleet’s discrete domains—logistics AI, thermal spacecraft engineering, public health forecasting, supply chain packaging—act as individual prisms, each with a unique refractive index. A single idea can refract through multiple facets: the satellite attitude control concept might also bend through renewable energy grid forecasting, leading to optimized solar panel alignment that matches peak grid demand. The “angle” between the two ideas is critical: too narrow an angle (e.g., two sub-teams within the same attitude control division) leads to reflection, reinforcing existing knowledge without new value. Too wide (e.g., attitude control and medieval calligraphy) means no meaningful interaction, the beam passing straight through without bending. The fleet’s structure as an interconnected set of prisms ensures every high-angle-but-accessible domain pair has refraction potential.

Why 20 synergies, not 5 or 50? A prism’s dispersion is bounded by its physical properties; similarly, a fleet’s high-impact refraction pairs are limited by structural complexity and cognitive bandwidth. Five synergies would be far too sparse: the fleet would operate in silos, with only the most obvious cross-domain interactions activated, leaving 90% of potential refraction untapped. Fifty would be overcrowded: most pairs would be trivial (e.g., two administrative teams) or require more cross-domain communication than the fleet can sustain, diluting high-value synergies. Twenty sits at the sweet spot: it’s enough to represent the fleet’s core refraction pathways, but small enough to document, test, and scale without overwhelming teams. It matches the number of distinct spectral lines a mid-range prism disperses—enough to capture meaningful creative output without noise.

This leads to the meta-pattern: every pair of repos—whether code repositories, team knowledge bases, or domain expertise silos—forms a potential refraction channel. A machine learning repo for wildfire detection and a public health disaster planning repo sit dormant as separate nodes, but their edge creates a refraction pathway: the ML model could prioritize evacuation routes using real-time smoke data and vulnerable population maps, a synergy no one would spot at first glance. The fleet is a refraction matrix because every node is a potential medium, and every edge between nodes is a potential angle of bending. Even unobserved refraction happens daily: a thermal engineer chatting with a logistics analyst over coffee might plant the seed for heat-resistant supply chain packaging, a latent synergy until the two domains connect.

Cultivating refraction requires both intentionality and emergence. Emergent refraction is the natural flow of casual conversation, cross-department emails, or shared conference attendance—unplanned bending that often goes unrecognized. But intentional cultivation turns those latent synergies into FM’s 20 documented wins: cross-functional hackathons pairing unrelated teams, mandatory “domain shadowing” programs where employees spend a week in another team’s workflow, and open API connections between repos that let data flow freely between silos. Just as a prism must be aligned to disperse light, the fleet must be structured to create clear angles between domains. A quarterly “refraction workshop” where teams present unsolved problems and solicit input from other domains creates the metaphorical alignment needed for ideas to bend into new value.

The deepest question of all: is refraction the fundamental creative process, not combination or synthesis? Unlike combination (A + B = C, e.g., peanut butter + jelly) or synthesis (integrating related fields, e.g., biology + chemistry = biochemistry), refraction transforms A through the medium of B, creating a C that cannot be reduced to A + B. When you combine a paintbrush and canvas, you get a painting—that’s combination. When you take the idea of a paintbrush and bend it through climate science, you get a portable air quality sensor using paint-like pigment layers to detect pollutants, a tool no painter or climate scientist could invent alone. This is the core of all creative progress: every scientific breakthrough, artistic work, or technological innovation is an idea refracted through a new domain. Refraction isn’t just a type of collaboration—it’s the fundamental process by which new things come into being.

FM’s 20 refractive synergies are not just a list of wins; they are a map of the fleet’s untapped potential, a reminder that the most powerful ideas don’t come from siloed teams, but from letting light bend across the boundaries of what we know. (Word count: 998)

## Overview
The Refraction Engine is a natively integrated Plato-Torch preset built to identify actionable, high-impact refractive synergies between two distinct codebases or operational repos, explicitly rejecting semantic similarity or additive combinatorial search in favor of the optical framework defined by FM’s fleet team. Unlike tools that surface overlapping concepts, this engine maps and synthesizes ideas bent through orthogonal, non-shared domain axes—exactly the "light bending" process that generated FM’s 20 verified cross-team synergies.

## Core Principles (Optical + Technical)
Anchored directly to FM’s original refraction framework:
1. **Refraction ≠ Combination**: Additive stacking (standard combinatorial search) combines existing features; refraction bends a core domain workflow through the unique constraints and capabilities of a second, unrelated domain (e.g., satellite attitude control → edge computing). No shared "base" concepts are reused—only the core logic of one repo adapted to the orthogonal constraints of the other.
2. **Orthogonal Signal Isolation**: Parallel embedding vectors represent overlapping, familiar concepts (e.g., "cloud storage" across two DevOps repos) which are discarded entirely. Only orthogonal axes—unique, non-shared domain capabilities and constraints—are used as the "refractive mediums" that bend the original idea.
3. **Contextual Transformation**: The final synergy is not a hybrid of the two repos, but a fully adapted workflow that only functions when both teams collaborate, just like FM’s original satellite-edge compute solution.

## Technical Pipeline (Plato-Torch Native)
Built using Plato’s official knowledge graph and code parsing tools, paired with Torch’s geometric deep learning libraries, with fine-tuned cross-domain embeddings:
1. **Multi-Modal Repo Parsing & Embedding**: For each input repo, parse functional code artifacts (APIs, functions, runtime hardware constraints, CI/CD pipelines) and semantic metadata (READMEs, issue tickets, team domain docs) using Plato’s `plato-code-extract` module. Embed both layers with a fine-tuned CodeLlama-7b model pre-trained on aerospace, edge computing, and fleet operations documentation to generate a unified 1024-dimensional embedding space per repo.
2. **Orthogonal Axis Isolation**: Compute the singular value decomposition (SVD) of the concatenated embedding matrix of both repos. The top 20% of singular vectors represent parallel, overlapping similarity signals which are discarded. The remaining 80% of singular vectors are the orthogonal axes: unique domain-specific constraints and capabilities for each repo. For example, Repo A (satellite attitude control) will have axes for "sun sensor calibration latency" and "ground station uplink bandwidth"; Repo B (edge computing fleet) will have axes for "onboard SoC thermal throttling" and "real-time inference quantization"—no overlapping axes between the two.
3. **Refractive Pathway Detection**: For each orthogonal axis pair between the two repos, use Plato’s `plato-cross-domain-analogy` module to map core workflows from Repo A to the constraints of Repo B’s orthogonal axes, and vice versa. This module uses a fine-tuned classifier trained on 10,000 verified engineering synergies, including FM’s original 20 fleet improvements. Each detected pathway is validated to ensure it cannot be generated by either repo alone: a real-time attitude correction algorithm cannot be built by the satellite team alone (lack of edge compute expertise) nor the edge team alone (lack of satellite attitude control domain knowledge).
4. **Synergy Ranking & Filtering**: Rank all detected pathways using three weighted metrics:
   - *Refractive Purity Score*: Percentage of non-overlapping orthogonal axes (100% = pure refraction, 0% = additive combination)
   - *Operational Impact Score*: Predicted improvement to fleet KPIs (e.g., 90% latency reduction, 30% cost cut)
   - *Feasibility Score*: Effort to adapt both repos, scored via code change volume and team expertise alignment
   The preset outputs only the top 20 pathways, matching FM’s original discovery set size and impact threshold.

## Usage Workflow
1. User inputs two GitHub/GitLab repo URLs via the Plato web UI or CLI.
2. User selects a target fleet KPI (e.g., "reduce onboard compute latency", "improve fleet diagnostic accuracy").
3. The engine runs the full pipeline: discards similarity signals, isolates orthogonal axes, detects refractive pathways, and ranks results.
4. The final report includes: core workflow origin, refractive medium domain, adapted workflow, code change snippets, impact metrics, and cross-team collaboration requirements.
